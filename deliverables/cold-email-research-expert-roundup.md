## Infrastructure Framework

### The Adjacent Domain Strategy
Don't send cold outbound from your main domain. Buy "adjacent domains" that are similar to your company's:

| Main Domain | Adjacent Domains |
|-------------|------------------|
| za-zu.com | za-zuemails.com, yesza-zu.com, withza-zu.com, getza-zu.com |

**Why?**
- If you get flagged for spam, your entire domain could get penalized
- ESPs flag high-volume sending from single accounts as suspicious
- Diversify sending across multiple domains to reduce risk

### Domain Volume Formula
```
Adjacent Domains = (Desired Daily Volume / 60) × 1.1
```

**Example calculations:**
- 60 emails/day → 1-2 domains
- 6,000 emails/day → 110 domains

The 1.1 multiplier ensures you have reserve domains for when active domains experience deliverability problems.

### Email Setup Standards
- **3 inboxes per domain** — prevents too many spam reports per domain
- **20 cold emails per inbox per day** — mimics human sending patterns
- **Use Google Workspace or Microsoft 365** — avoid private IP infrastructure
- **301 redirect adjacent domains** to main domain so visitors hit your real website

### Inbox Warmup Protocol
**Before sending any cold outbound:**
1. Warm inboxes for 3-4 weeks minimum
2. Start slowly (ramp up to 20 emails over 2 weeks, not day 1)
3. Use tools that warm from the SAME IP you'll send from
4. Maintain warming even after outbound starts (adjust volume based on campaign performance)

**What good warmup looks like:**
- Emails sent to accounts that engage positively
- Opening emails, marking as important
- Replying on occasion
- Marking emails as "not spam" if they hit spam
- Copy should match your cold email topics (not generic "Office party at 6pm?")

## Email Frameworks & Principles

### Rule of 20
Each inbox should send no more than 20 cold emails per day. This matches what a normal human would send and keeps ESPs from flagging suspicious activity.

### Domain Health Monitoring
Track deliverability across domains. When one domain's performance degrades:
1. Swap it out with a warmed reserve domain
2. Investigate what caused the deliverability drop
3. Re-warm the problematic domain before using again

## The Push-Pull Method

### What It Is
A framework balancing two psychological forces:
- **PUSH** — Creates urgency, highlights your agenda
- **PULL** — Engages recipient's interest, draws them closer

### Structure
```
1. Start with PULL (make it about them)
2. Introduce PUSH (your agenda)
3. Maintain engagement (balance throughout)
```

### The PULL (Opening)
**Purpose:** Draw the reader in by making the email about them

**Techniques:**
- Comment on a recent achievement
- Express genuine interest in their work
- Highlight specific details from research
- Compliment their work specifically

**Example Pull Openers:**
```
"Saw your post about [specific topic] — 
the point about [specific insight] really 
resonated with me..."

"Your team's expansion into [market] is 
impressive — especially how you approached 
[specific challenge]..."
```

### The PUSH (Your Ask)
**Purpose:** Clearly communicate what you want

**Techniques:**
- Align your ask with their interests
- Be clear and concise
- Use "soft asks" — single, specific actions
- Offer choices to respect autonomy

**Example Push Asks:**
```
"Worth a quick conversation to explore if 
this could help with [their challenge]?"

"Would it be helpful to see how [similar 
company] solved this?"

"Open to a 15-minute chat?"
```

## Personalization to Relevance Framework

### The Problem
- **Personalization without relevance:** Prospect LIKES you (you did research) but won't BUY (doesn't see how you can help)
- **Relevance without personalization:** "Low hanging fruit" may buy, but most ignore mass templates

### The Solution: Hook Them Together
```
Personalization (research) → Bridge → Relevance (why it matters)
```

### Framework Structure
1. **True Personalization:** Research that shows genuine interest
2. **True Relevance:** Connect to a pain point or priority
3. **The Hook:** Tie personalization to relevance seamlessly

**Example:**
```
Hi [Name],

[PERSONALIZATION - specific research]
Saw you're hiring SDRs this quarter and 
expanding into EMEA.

[RELEVANCE - pain point]
Most sales leaders I talk to worry about 
how to maintain message quality when 
scaling across time zones.

[HOOK - bridge to solution]
We helped [similar company] keep reply 
rates consistent while tripling headcount.

Worth exploring?
```

## The Infrastructure Playbook

### Phase 1: Domain Strategy

**Step 1: Buy Adjacent Domains**
- Similar to main domain (e.g., get[company].com, [company]hq.com)
- Buy from Cloudflare, GoDaddy, etc.
- Buy 10-20% more than you think you need

**Step 2: Set Up DNS Records**
- **SPF (Sender Policy Framework):** Authorizes sending IPs
- **DKIM (DomainKeys Identified Mail):** Cryptographic signature
- **DMARC (Domain-based Message Authentication):** Policy for handling failed authentication

**Step 3: 301 Redirect to Main Domain**
```
za-zuhq.com → za-zu.com
withza-zu.com → za-zu.com
```

### Phase 2: Inbox Setup

**Configuration:**
- 3 inboxes per domain
- Google Workspace or Microsoft 365 (NOT private IP)
- Real names (founders/leaders work best)
- Profile pictures included

**Account Creation Tips:**
- Use VPNs and rotate IPs
- Use antidetect browsers (Adspower, Wavebox)
- Use temporary phone numbers (TextVerified)

### Phase 3: Inbox Warming

**Before Outbound (3-4 weeks):**
- Start slow, ramp up to 20 emails/day
- Use warming tools with high-quality pools
- Ensure copy matches your actual campaign topics

**During Outbound:**
- Maintain warming (reduced volume)
- Monitor engagement rates
- Have warmed reserve inboxes ready

**Warming Quality Indicators:**
- Pool quality (no spam traps)
- Email frequency (gradual ramp)
- Copy similarity (matches campaigns)
- Randomization (varied engagement patterns)

## Writing Great Emails

### Email Structure
```
1. Observation (personalization)
2. Problem/Insight (relevance)
3. Credibility (proof)
4. Solution (value prop)
5. Call-to-Conversation (CTC, not CTA)
```

### Key Principles

**1. One Idea Per Email**
> "Clear is kind. Clarity is how you write emails that get replies."

**2. 25-50 Words Optimal**
Data shows the best emails are short. Long emails = instant delete.

**3. Mobile-First**
- 8x more likely first impression is on mobile
- 3x more likely you're writing on desktop
- This disconnect kills replies

**4. Binary Questions**
Ask yes/no questions, not open-ended ones:
- ❌ "What's your current process for X?"
- ✅ "Is improving X a priority this quarter?"

**5. No "Educating"**
> "They didn't open their inbox to get a lecture."

### Email Examples

**The Observation → Question:**
```
Subject: SDR ramp at [Company]

Will, looks like you're hiring reps.

Would it be helpful to get a more granular 
look at how they're ramping on email?
```

**The Credibility Build:**
```
Subject: [Company] outbound

Hi [Name],

Saw you're expanding your sales team.

Most companies we work with struggle with
maintaining quality while scaling volume.

We helped [Customer] increase reply rates
by 40% while tripling their outbound.

Worth a chat to compare notes?

[Signature]
```

## The Mental Spam Filter

### The Concept
Your recipient's brain has a spam filter more powerful than Gmail's:
- **11 seconds** — average time spent reading an email
- **Categorize, don't comprehend** — first question: "what is this?"
- **Pattern recognition** — if it looks like a sales email, it's deleted

### 6 Things That Trigger the Filter

| Trigger | Problem | Fix |
|---------|---------|-----|
| **Marketing Subject Lines** | "Will, are you trying to increase replies?" | Make it boring, topical, feel internal |
| **Clichés** | "I hope this finds you well" | Never use these phrases |
| **Length** | Too long = too much work | 25-50 words optimal |
| **Buzzwords** | "Strategic revenue enablement" | Plain English, concrete |
| **"Educating"** | Lecturing the buyer | Don't talk at them |
| **Bad Formatting** | Big paragraphs on mobile | Short lines, mobile-first |

### The Psychology of Cold Email
> "Your recipient isn't reading your message the way you wrote it. They read it through the lens of their own psychology."

## 13 Psychology Tools for Cold Email

### 1. Cognitive Overload
**Principle:** Too much information = instant exit
**Application:** Trim until almost too simple. Short sentences. Zero fluff.

### 2. Heuristic Substitution
**Principle:** Hard questions get replaced with easy ones
- Hard: "Is this relevant and worth my time?"
- Easy: "Does this look like something I already ignore?"
**Application:** Look different. Visually feel worth reading.

### 3. Loss Aversion
**Principle:** Losses feel heavier than gains
**Application:** Highlight missed opportunities, slowdowns, gaps. Don't threaten.

### 4. Reciprocity
**Principle:** Effort begets effort
**Application:** Use real research. Not name-drops — actual evidence you thought about them.

### 5. Cognitive Miser
**Principle:** Brain loves shortcuts
**Application:** Break the pattern. Force receiver to reevaluate.

### 6. Confirmation Bias
**Principle:** Interpret through lens of existing beliefs
**Application:** Signal your message was crafted with care. Call out differentiators.

### 7. Ambiguity Aversion
**Principle:** Unclear → No action
**Application:** Make your point concrete. Ask simple, direct questions.

### 8. Friction Avoidance
**Principle:** Bigger ask = smaller response
**Application:** Ask a tiny question. One small thing answerable in 10 seconds.

### 9. Von Restorff Effect
**Principle:** Things that stand out are remembered
**Application:** Use one distinct element — metaphor, surprising sentence, wonky word.

### 10. Attentional Bias
**Principle:** Focus only on current priorities
**Application:** Tie outreach to what's happening now — context, triggers, active pain.

### 11. The Mum Effect
**Principle:** Avoid sending bad news upward
**Application:** Make narrative safe. Frame environment as changing, not them as "wrong."

### 12. Reactance
**Principle:** Push harder, they pull away
**Application:** Remove pressure. Use permission, curiosity, optionality.

### 13. Power Conservation Theory
**Principle:** Status quo protects those in power
**Application:** Make suggestion feel incremental, not revolutionary.

## 16 Email Frameworks

### 1. The Mouse Trap
**Use case:** First touch, DM, Follow-up
**Structure:**
1. Observation
2. Question (value-prop driven, binary)

**Example:**
```
Will, looks like you're hiring reps.

Would it be helpful to get a more granular 
look at how they're ramping up on email?
```

### 2. The Mouse Trap + Context
**Use case:** First touch, DM, Follow-up
**Structure:**
1. Observation
2. Question
3. Context

**Example:**
```
Will, looks like you're hiring reps.

Would it be helpful to get a more granular 
look at how they're ramping on email?

Our in-inbox email coach, Lavender, helps 
teams ramp faster.
```

### 3. Vanilla Ice Cream
**Use case:** First touch (foundational framework)
**Structure:**
1. Observation (personalization)
2. Problem/Insight (implied pain point)
3. Credibility (customer proof)
4. Solution (what you did)
5. Call-to-Conversation (CTC)

**Example:**
```
George,

I saw you're hiring SDRs. I imagine you're 
thinking about how they'll ramp.

Usually, our customers focus on cold calls. 
But their email results lag.

We're helping reps at Sendoso ramp faster. 
They continue to improve each quarter.

Worth a chat?

Will
```

### 4. Neutral Insights
**Use case:** Follow up, LinkedIn DMs, nurture
**Structure:**
1. Reference third-party resource
2. Explain why they should read
3. Explain why you're sharing
4. Optional soft ask

**Example:**
```
George, do you read Outreach's blog?

Given you're likely ramping reps, I thought 
you'd find it interesting.

The VP of Sales Dev at Segment wrote about 
how she scaled her team to a $3.2B acquisition.

Check it out

Will
P.S. Any thoughts on my last note?
```

### 5. The Thoughtful Bump
**Use case:** Follow up
**Structure:** Context + Bump (1-3 sentences)

**Example:**
```
Hey George,

Given that you're growing the sales team, 
I thought this would be worth discussing.

Did you have any feedback on my note?

Will
```

### 6. The Clarification
**Use case:** Follow up
**Structure:**
1. Restate context
2. Clarify "what we do"
3. Clarify "why that's relevant"
4. Call-to-conversation

**Example:**
```
Hey George,

Given you're growing the sales team, I thought 
coaching might be top of mind.

To clarify, we've built a sales email coach. 
Sits right inside their inbox.

High-growth teams like Twilio get great results.

Think this could help your team?

Will
```

### 7. The Well-Researched Referral
**Use case:** Follow up, need internal referral
**Structure:**
1. Start with who (namedrop)
2. Ask if they're the better person
3. Explain why they might be
4. Explain why you reached out originally

**Example:**
```
Hey Will,

Would Jen be a better person to talk to 
about this webinar?

Realize she manages community activity. 
Given the event is all sellers, I thought 
you'd want the exposure.

Will
```

### 8. The Breakup
**Use case:** Final email in sequence
**Structure:**
1. State you've reached out
2. Explain why
3. Assume timing is off
4. Ask them to correct you
5. Clarify it's the last email

**Example:**
```
Hey George,

I've reached out a few times because you're 
hiring so many new reps.

I thought our email coach could help.

I'm going to chalk it up to my timing being 
off. Let me know if I'm wrong, but I'll 
stop my outreach for now.

Do you think this will be a priority in the 
next two quarters?
```

### 9. The Competitor
**Use case:** First touch with known competitor user
**Structure:**
1. Acknowledge competitor
2. Ask if they're happy
3. Compliment competitor
4. Highlight shortcomings
5. Open dialogue with question

**Example:**
```
Hey Will,

Saw you're using Drift for chat. How are 
you liking it?

I know their product is killer for lead gen. 
However, customers switch to us because they 
need more functionality for customer support.

Could you see chat playing a factor in 
reducing churn?
```

### 10. The Video

1. Observation (context)
2. Tie observation to challenge
3. Include video
4. Call-to-conversation

**Example:**

find this interesting. Growth typically leads 
to one of two things: standardizing or risking 
no visibility across inboxes.

Assuming you're personalizing emails, thought

Will

## Outbound Playbook for 2025

### The Four Pillars
1. **Cold Calling** — yes, it still works
2. **Email Campaigns** — automated sequences
3. **LinkedIn Campaigns** — signal-based engagement
4. **Manual Prospecting** — Tier 1 accounts

### ICP Scoring Framework

**Step 1: Define Your ICP**
- Company characteristics
- Buyer personas
- Trigger events

**Step 2: Build Scoring Model**
- Firmographics (size, location, industry)
- Technographics (tools used)
- Funding/growth signals
- Intent signals

**Step 3: Tier Your Accounts**
```
Dream 150:   Manual LinkedIn prospecting
Tier 1:      Cold calling + semi-automated sequences
Tier 2:      Automated email + LinkedIn sequences
Tier 3:      Automated email sequences
```

### When to Reach Out

2. User hits "aha moment" in product
3. User visits high-intent pages
4. Account approaching usage limits
5. User starts trial of premium feature

# Synthesis: Common Themes Across All 5 Experts

## 1. Infrastructure First
**All five experts emphasize deliverability as table stakes.**
- Eric & Matt: Extensive domain/inbox infrastructure
- Will: Mental spam filter as real as Gmail's
- Kyle: Automation tools depend on good infrastructure
- Becc: Relevance won't matter if emails don't arrive

**Action:** Buy adjacent domains, set up SPF/DKIM/DMARC, warm inboxes for 3-4 weeks before sending.

## 2. Frameworks > Templates
**Every expert advocates understanding structure over copying text.**
- Will: "We give you frameworks, not templates"
- Becc: Push-Pull method, personalization-to-relevance
- Eric: Systematic approaches to research and sending
- Matt: Evidence-backed methods with testing
- Kyle: Signal categories as reusable frameworks

**Action:** Learn the underlying structure. Understand WHY frameworks work, not just what to say.

## 3. Personalization + Relevance
**The combination is necessary; neither alone works.**
- Becc: Personalization without relevance = liked but not bought
- Will: Reciprocity requires real research, not name-drops
- Eric: AI-powered research for genuine personalization
- Matt: Evidence-backed personalization at scale
- Kyle: Signal-based plays tie context to timing

**Action:** Research prospects genuinely. Tie research to their challenges. Don't fake it.

## 4. Psychology Is the Unlock
**Understanding how buyers think beats clever copy.**
- Will: 13 specific psychology principles
- Becc: Push-Pull based on psychology of urgency/attraction
- Matt: Cognitive load, friction avoidance
- Eric: Pattern recognition triggers spam filters

**Action:** Study cognitive biases. Apply them to email structure. Remove friction.

## 5. Shorter Is Better
**All experts converge on brevity.**
- Will: 25-50 words optimal
- Matt: One idea per email
- Becc: Make every word earn its place
- Eric: Quick observations, binary questions

**Action:** Cut ruthlessly. If a word doesn't add value, delete it.

## 6. Mobile-First Mindset
**8x more likely first impression on mobile.**
- Will: Major cause of dying conversations
- Matt: Format for mobile, not desktop
- Eric: Plain text often outperforms HTML

**Action:** Write short paragraphs. Test on mobile. Assume they're skimming.

## 7. Signals Beat Spray
**Intent-based outreach outperforms volume-based.**
- Kyle: 25 signal-based plays
- Becc: ~200 trigger categories in Encyclopedia
- Eric: AI to identify signal-worthy prospects
- Matt: Tier allocation based on fit + signals

**Action:** Build signal detection systems. Outreach when prospects show intent.

## 8. Testing & Iteration
**All advocate continuous experimentation.**
- Matt: "Test, measure, optimize"
- Will: A/B test everything, use data
- Eric: Track by domain/inbox, rotate underperformers
- Kyle: Run small tests before scaling

**Action:** Test subject lines, openers, CTAs. Track by segment. Iterate based on data.

## 9. Tools Are Enablers, Not Solutions
**Expertise + process > any single tool.**
- All recommend tool stacks but emphasize understanding
- Infrastructure matters more than the specific vendor
- AI helps scale but doesn't replace thinking

**Action:** Build process first. Add tools to accelerate. Don't expect tools to fix bad strategy.

## 10. The Human Element
**Despite automation focus, all emphasize humanity.**
- Becc: Buyers are people with metrics and pressures
- Will: Remove pressure, use curiosity
- Eric: Use real names, real research
- Kyle: Personalized assistance pairs with automation
- Matt: Write like a human, not a marketer

**Action:** Sound like a person. Show genuine interest. Don't pitch — solve.

# Quick Reference: Subject Line Rules

1. **No marketing speak** — avoid verbs, commands, first names in subjects
2. **Boring is better** — make it feel internal
3. **Topic over pitch** — describe what it's about, not what you want
4. **Short** — 3-5 words maximum
5. **Test variations** — subject lines are easiest to A/B test
