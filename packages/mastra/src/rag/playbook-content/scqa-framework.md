# SCQA Framework

## Overview

The SCQA framework is a structured communication method that helps presenters organize their message in a way that captures attention and drives action. SCQA stands for **Situation**, **Complication**, **Question**, and **Answer**.

## Structure

### Situation
Establish the context and what everyone already knows. This should be uncontroversial and something your audience agrees with. The goal is to get buy-in on the foundation before introducing anything new.

Example: "Our company has been the market leader in the enterprise software space for the past decade."

### Complication
Introduce the problem, challenge, or change that creates tension. This is what disrupts the status quo and makes the audience sit up and pay attention.

Example: "However, new cloud-native competitors have emerged with significantly lower cost structures."

### Question
Pose the central question that your presentation will answer. This frames the rest of your communication and gives the audience a clear purpose for listening.

Example: "How do we maintain our market leadership while adapting to this shifting landscape?"

### Answer
Provide your answer or recommendation. This is the core of your message and should receive the most attention in your presentation.

Example: "We need to invest in a cloud-native transformation of our core platform within the next 18 months."

## Key Principles

1. **Start with agreement**: The Situation should be something your audience already accepts. This builds momentum.

2. **Create tension**: The Complication should genuinely challenge the status quo. Without tension, there's no need for change.

3. **Make it specific**: The Question should be clear and focused. Avoid vague or rhetorical questions.

4. **Lead with the answer**: In most business contexts, audiences prefer to hear the answer early (the "answer-first" approach).

5. **Support with evidence**: After presenting your Answer, support it with data, examples, and reasoning.

## When to Use SCQA

- Executive presentations
- Strategic proposals
- Change management communications
- Problem-solving narratives
- Any communication requiring buy-in for a recommended action

## Examples in Practice

### Sales Pitch
- **Situation**: Your company has been growing 20% annually
- **Complication**: Market saturation is limiting further growth in current segments
- **Question**: How do you unlock the next phase of growth?
- **Answer**: Expand into the SMB segment with a self-service product tier

### Internal Proposal
- **Situation**: Our customer satisfaction scores have been stable at 85%
- **Complication**: Competitors are now achieving 92% satisfaction with AI-powered support
- **Question**: How can we close this gap and improve customer retention?
- **Answer**: Implement an AI support assistant to provide 24/7 instant responses

### Status Update
- **Situation**: We launched the new dashboard feature last quarter
- **Complication**: Adoption is only 40% versus our 80% target
- **Question**: What can we do to drive higher adoption?
- **Answer**: Launch an in-app onboarding flow and provide targeted training

## Common Mistakes

1. **Skipping the Situation**: Jumping straight to the problem without establishing context
2. **Weak Complication**: The problem isn't compelling enough to demand action
3. **Vague Question**: The question is too broad or unclear
4. **Delayed Answer**: Burying the recommendation too late in the presentation
5. **Missing Connection**: The Answer doesn't clearly address the Complication
