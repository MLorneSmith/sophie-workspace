# Minto Framework

## Overview

The Minto Framework, developed by Barbara Minto, is a methodology for structuring logical arguments in business communications. It builds upon the Pyramid Principle but adds specific techniques for developing and presenting compelling arguments that drive decision-making.

## The Minto Pyramid

The framework uses a specific type of pyramid structure where:

1. **The Situation** forms the base
2. **The Complication** creates tension
3. **The Question** frames the decision
4. **The Answer** (your recommendation) sits at the top

This is essentially the SCQA framework applied within the pyramid structure.

## Core Principles

### Principle 1: Start with the Answer
The most important information should come first. Don't make your audience wait to understand your recommendation.

### Principle 2: Support with Reasons
Every recommendation must be supported by clear, logical reasons. These reasons should be:
- Logically ordered (cause → effect, or importance order)
- Mutually exclusive
- Collectively exhaustive

### Principle 3: Use Inductive Reasoning
When presenting multiple supporting points, group them so that the reader can see the logical progression:
- Problem → Cause → Solution
- Observation → Interpretation → Action
- Fact → Implication → Recommendation

## Argument Development

### The Minto Sequence

1. **State the Recommendation**
   Start with your conclusion or recommendation. Be specific and actionable.

2. **Present Supporting Reasons**
   List 2-7 reasons that justify your recommendation. Each reason should be:
   - A complete thought
   - Logically connected to the recommendation
   - Independent (you could remove any one and still have a valid argument)

3. **Provide Evidence for Each Reason**
   Back each reason with:
   - Data or facts
   - Examples or case studies
   - Analysis or reasoning
   - Expert opinions

### Example Argument Structure

**Recommendation**: Launch the beta program in March.

**Reason 1**: Market timing is optimal
- Q2 competitors are focused on their annual releases
- Customer advisory board members have requested early access
- Industry conference in April provides launch opportunity

**Reason 2**: Technical readiness
- Core features are complete and stable
- Infrastructure can support 500 beta users
- Internal testing shows 95% success rate

**Reason 3**: Resource availability
- Engineering team has capacity after Q1 milestones
- Marketing has budget allocated for beta launch
- Customer success team is trained on new features

## The Minto Question

In the Minto Framework, the "question" is critical. Before presenting your answer, you must ensure:

1. The question is clear and specific
2. Your answer directly addresses the question
3. The audience understands what decision they're being asked to make

### Types of Minto Questions

1. **Yes/No Questions**
   "Should we proceed with the acquisition?"

2. **Which Questions**
   "Which option provides the best ROI?"

3. **How Questions**
   "How should we structure the partnership?"

4. **How Many/Much Questions**
   "How much should we invest in R&D?"

## Structuring Complex Arguments

### Nested Pyramids

For complex topics, use multiple levels of pyramids:

**Level 1**: Overall recommendation
**Level 2**: Supporting recommendations for each key area
**Level 3**: Evidence for each supporting recommendation

### Branching Arguments

When you have multiple audiences with different concerns:
- Identify the primary audience
- Address their key question first
- Include secondary arguments for other stakeholders
- Cross-reference where relevant

## Practical Application

### Writing the Opening

Begin with the answer, then preview your supporting structure:

> "I recommend we launch the mobile app in Q3 for three reasons: market readiness, technical feasibility, and competitive positioning. First, let me explain why market timing is critical..."

### Transitioning Between Points

Use clear transitions that reinforce the structure:

- "Beyond the market opportunity..."
- "In addition to technical readiness..."
- "Equally important is..."
- "The third factor is..."

### Closing

Restate the recommendation and the key reasons, then add a clear call to action:

> "Based on market timing, technical readiness, and resource availability, I recommend we launch in March. This positions us ahead of competitors and maximizes our Q3 revenue potential. Do I have your approval to proceed with the launch plan?"

## Common Pitfalls

1. **Starting with problem description** instead of the recommendation
2. **Using deductive reasoning** (premises → conclusion) instead of inductive
3. **Weak reasons** that don't logically lead to the recommendation
4. **Too many reasons** creating cognitive overload
5. **Missing evidence** for stated reasons
6. **Inconsistent parallel structure** across supporting points

## When to Use the Minto Framework

- Strategic recommendations
- Investment proposals
- Product launch decisions
- Partnership evaluations
- Any argument requiring buy-in from decision-makers

## Relationship to Other Frameworks

The Minto Framework is the foundational methodology. It provides:

- The **argument structure** for the Pyramid Principle
- The **logical development** for SCQA
- The **reasoning process** for the STAR Method
