### GPT-5.2 (openai-codex/gpt-5.2)

**Position:** Santa Claus is not real as a literal, physical being, but he *is* real as a cultural and psychological phenomenon with genuine social power.

**Argument:**
Literally and historically, there isn't a single immortal man living at the North Pole who delivers billions of gifts in one night; taken as a physical claim about the world, "Santa" is not real in that straightforward sense. But many of the elements behind Santa *are* real: a lineage of stories tied to Saint Nicholas and centuries of evolving folklore, plus the very real social practices—rituals, songs, symbols, and traditions—that make December feel different across families and communities.

Philosophically and psychologically, Santa functions as a shared narrative that shapes behavior and meaning. For children, he can be a concrete way to learn about generosity, anticipation, trust, and even moral imagination; for adults, he becomes a cultural shorthand for a season of giving that can prompt genuine acts of kindness and community-building. In that sense, Santa is "real" like money, nations, or holidays are real: not as a natural object, but as a powerful social construct that exists because people collectively uphold it—and its effects (joy, charity, cohesion, sometimes stress or consumerism) are measurable.

### GLM 4.7 (zai/glm-4.7)

**Position:** Santa Claus is real—not as a literal person, but as a cultural and psychological truth that manifests through shared belief, tradition, and the tangible acts of generosity he inspires.

**Argument:**
The question of Santa Claus's reality depends entirely on which dimension of "real" we're examining. Literally, as a single physical individual residing at the North Pole with flying reindeer, Santa Claus does not exist—that story, rooted in the historical Saint Nicholas of Myra, has evolved through centuries of folklore and commercialization into something else entirely. Yet culturally and psychologically, Santa is profoundly real. He exists as a shared narrative that transcends geography, unites generations through ritual, and embodies virtues—generosity, kindness, the joy of giving—that manifest concretely through human action. Millions of children experience Santa as real through their belief, and that subjective experience produces tangible effects: anticipation, wonder, and eventually the bittersweet rite of passage into knowing the secret while preserving it for others.

The philosophical question becomes whether existence requires material verification, or whether shared meaning, emotional impact, and behavioral influence constitute a form of reality that matters. Santa exists as a *real* cultural force—parents become his agents, children his believers, society collectively sustains the story through ritual, media, and tradition. The gifts under the tree are real; the love they represent is real; the memory formed is real. Santa Claus is real in the same way that justice, hope, and love are real—not as objects you can touch, but as ideas that shape how people move through the world and treat each other. The story persists because it serves something deeper in human nature: the need for magic, for mystery, for a figure who gives without expectation of return.

### Claude Opus 4.5 (anthropic/claude-opus-4-5)

**Position:** Santa Claus is real in every way that matters. The only sense in which he is "not real" is the narrowest literal one—and that is the least interesting dimension of reality to consider.

**Argument:**
The question of Santa Claus's reality invites us to examine what we mean by "real" itself. In the strictest literal sense, there is no immortal gift-giving man living at the North Pole with flying reindeer and a workshop of elves. But to stop there would be philosophically lazy. Consider: is justice real? Is love real? Is money real? These are social constructs that exist because we collectively believe in them and act accordingly—and their effects on the world are profound and measurable. Santa Claus belongs to this category of constructed reality. He exists as a cultural institution, a shared narrative that coordinates behavior across millions of households, shapes childhood experiences, and generates genuine emotional responses. The anticipation, the wonder, the moral framework of "naughty or nice"—these psychological effects are undeniably real.

Furthermore, Santa represents something philosophically significant: the embodiment of unconditional generosity and the magic of giving without expectation of return. Every parent who stays up late wrapping presents, every stranger who drops coins in a Salvation Army bucket, every anonymous benefactor—they are all instantiating the Santa archetype. In this sense, Santa is not one person but a distributed consciousness, a role that humans step into and perform. The character is a vessel for our better impulses toward generosity, wonder, and care for others. Children eventually learn "the truth," but many describe this not as disillusionment but as invitation—they graduate from believing in Santa to *becoming* Santa, perpetuating the reality through their own actions.
