**Subject line options:**

**Preview text:** It's not about prettier slides. It's about clearer thinking.

---

Most consulting presentations are forgotten within 48 hours.

Not because the analysis was wrong. Not because the slides were ugly. Because the story didn't land.

I've sat on both sides — building decks at Oliver Wyman and Mastercard Advisors, then buying consulting work across Mastercard's Cyber & Intelligence division. The presentations that changed decisions had one thing in common: you could state the recommendation in one sentence, and every slide built toward it.

The ones that didn't? Plenty of data. Beautiful charts. No narrative spine.

**Try this right now.** Open your last client deck. Find the recommendation slide. Can you state its argument in 12 words or fewer? If the slide hedges, lists options without a point of view, or buries the insight under caveats — the thinking wasn't done.

I call this the One Slide Test. Not simplification. Clarity of thought. The best consultants I've worked with always passed it.

## Email 2: Why AI-Generated Slides Feel Wrong (And What to Do Instead)

**Subject line options:**
- "AI made your slides faster. Did it make them better?"
- "Why AI-generated slides feel wrong"
- "The part AI skips (and why it matters)"

**Preview text:** The problem isn't the output. It's what happens before it.

---

You've tried the tools. Gamma. Beautiful.ai. Tome. ChatGPT with a slide plugin.

The output looks fine. Clean layouts. Reasonable icons. And something feels off. The deck looks like a deck but doesn't feel like *your* thinking — a summary of a summary, correct enough to present, too generic to persuade.

Every AI presentation tool optimizes for the same thing: turning text into visual layouts. They're good at that. What they skip is deciding what to say and in what order.

Structure isn't formatting. Structure is argument. It's knowing your client's real objection is cost, not timeline, and sequencing evidence accordingly. It's understanding the CFO needs the business case on slide 3, not slide 12. No AI tool knows that.

**What works instead: the 3-step pre-AI process.**

1. **State the one thing.** Before you touch any tool, write one sentence: "After this presentation, the client will decide to ___." If you can't fill in the blank, you're not ready to build slides.

2. **Sketch the argument.** Five bullet points, in order. Each one should answer: "Why should the client believe the previous point?" This is your narrative skeleton.

3. **Now use AI.** Feed it the skeleton. Let it handle layout, formatting, visual hierarchy. That's what it's good at. Don't let it handle thinking. That's what you're good at.

The consultants who get the most from AI tools use them last — after the thinking is done.

## Email 3: The Framework McKinsey Uses (That Nobody Teaches)

**Subject line options:**
- "The framework behind every great consulting deck"
- "SCQA: the structure nobody teaches"
- "McKinsey's presentation secret isn't design"

**Preview text:** Pyramid Principle is mentioned everywhere. Almost nobody applies it correctly.

---

Everyone in consulting has heard of the Pyramid Principle. Barbara Minto's framework gets referenced in every strategy training and slide deck workshop.

Almost nobody uses it correctly.

What usually happens: someone reads "start with the answer," puts their recommendation on slide 2, and calls it structured thinking. That's not the Pyramid Principle. That's just moving a slide.

The real power is SCQA — Situation, Complication, Question, Answer. A narrative framework, not a slide order.

**A real example.** You're presenting to a retail bank about their fraud detection system.

**Without SCQA:** "We analyzed your fraud detection capabilities across 12 dimensions. Here are our findings organized by category. On the next 30 slides, we'll walk through each dimension. Our recommendation is on slide 34."

**With SCQA:**
- **Situation:** "Your fraud losses are within industry benchmarks. Your detection system catches 94% of known fraud patterns."
- **Complication:** "But fraud is shifting. Account takeover attacks increased 340% last year. Your system was built for card-present fraud — it has a blind spot for digital-first attack vectors."
- **Question:** "How do you close the digital fraud gap without rebuilding your entire detection stack?"
- **Answer:** "Three targeted investments — real-time behavioral analytics, device fingerprinting integration, and a dedicated digital fraud team — that close 80% of the gap within 6 months, at 30% of the cost of a full rebuild."

The client is leaning forward by the Complication. By the Answer, they're asking how fast you can start. That's what structure does.

## Email 4: I Watched 200 Consultant Presentations. Here's What Separates Good from Great.

**Subject line options:**
- "200 presentations later, here's what I learned"
- "The 3 habits of great presenters (and 3 of terrible ones)"
- "What I noticed after 200 consulting presentations"

**Preview text:** Two decades. Two patterns. A checklist you can steal.

---

Twenty years. Oliver Wyman, Mastercard Advisors, Mastercard Cyber & Intelligence. Hundreds of presentations — given, received, reviewed, survived.

Patterns emerge.

**What the great ones do:**

**1. Every slide has a so-what.** Not a title — a so-what. "Revenue grew 12%" is a title. "Revenue growth is decelerating — act in Q3" is a so-what. Read just the titles in order. They should tell the whole story.

**2. Evidence follows a hierarchy.** Strongest proof first. Not chronologically, not by data source — by persuasive weight. If the financial analysis convinces, lead with it. Don't bury it behind market sizing they already accept.

**3. Deliberate repetition.** The recommendation appears in the executive summary, the section transition, and the closing. Three times minimum. Attention is fragile. Repetition is respect.

**What the mediocre ones do:**

**1. Data dumps.** Forty slides with no argument connecting them. Unspoken message: "We did a lot of work. Please be impressed."

**2. Buried insights.** The recommendation is on slide 28. Everything before it is throat-clearing.

**3. The "any questions?" ending.** The weakest close. Great presentations end with a specific ask: "We recommend Option B. We need approval to begin Phase 1 by March 15th."

**Steal this checklist.** Before your next client presentation, ask:

- [ ] Can I read just the slide titles and understand the full argument?
- [ ] Is my strongest evidence in the first third?
- [ ] Does my recommendation appear at least three times?
- [ ] Does my last slide have a specific ask with a date?
- [ ] Could I present this in half the slides without losing the argument?
- [ ] Have I removed every slide that exists only to show I did work?

## Email 5: The Bottleneck Isn't Your Slides — It's Your Process

**Subject line options:**
- "You're spending 4 hours on a 90-minute deck"
- "The bottleneck isn't your slides"
- "Stop iterating in PowerPoint"

**Preview text:** The problem isn't speed. It's where you start.

---

You get a new engagement. Open PowerPoint. Start building slides — from a template, a previous deck, scratch. Move things around. Adjust the layout. Wordsmith a bullet point. Three hours later, 15 slides and no clear argument.

So you restructure. Move section 3 before section 2. Rewrite the exec summary. Another hour. Send it for review. Colleague suggests a different angle. Back to restructuring.

Four hours. Maybe five. For a deck that should take 90 minutes.

The bottleneck isn't PowerPoint or your design skills. It's that you're iterating on slides when you should be iterating on thinking.

**The Napkin First method:**

Before you open any tool, write five things:

1. Who is the audience and what do they care about?
2. What is the one decision I want them to make?
3. What is the strongest reason they should?
4. What is their biggest objection?
5. How do I address that objection?

Five lines. Three minutes. Now you know what your deck needs to say before you worry about how it looks.

This is the gap we're building SlideHeroes to close. Not faster slides — better thinking *before* slides. A tool that forces the napkin step, applies consulting-grade structure, and only then generates visual output.

More on that next week. Try the napkin method on your next deck. You'll be surprised how fast the slides come together when the thinking is already done.
